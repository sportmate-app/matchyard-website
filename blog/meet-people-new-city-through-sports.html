How to meet people in a new city through sports

A 30-day plan for turning an empty calendar into a standing group of people you actually look forward to seeing.

MatchYard·8 min read·Updated July 2026

The most reliable way to meet people in a new city isn't a networking event or an app swipe — it's a recurring, low-pressure activity that puts you in front of the same faces every week. Sports are close to perfect for this: they give you a reason to show up, a shared focus so you don't have to force conversation, and natural repetition, which is the actual mechanism behind every friendship you've ever made.

Here's why it works, and a 30-day plan to go from knowing no one to having a standing game on the calendar.

Why sport beats every other way to meet people

Friendship research keeps landing on the same finding: closeness is mostly a function of repeated, unforced time together. One study put casual friendship at roughly 40–60 hours of shared time, and closer friendship at a lot more. The problem after a move isn't that you're unlikable — it's that you have no structure that produces those hours. Sport builds the structure for you:

  • A built-in reason. You're there to play, so you never have to justify why you showed up or manufacture small talk.
  • A shared focus. A ball or a route does the social heavy lifting; conversation happens sideways, which is exactly when it's easiest.
  • Automatic repetition. The same run, the same court, the same class — you see people again and again without scheduling anything.
  • Instant common ground. You already know one true thing you have in common. That's more than most first meetings start with.
You don't make friends by trying to make friends. You make friends by repeatedly showing up to the same thing until the strangers become regulars.

The 30-day plan

Week 1 — Lower the bar and just show up

Your only goal this week is to attend one thing. A drop-in class, a pickup game, a beginner-friendly run club — pick the lowest-commitment option you can find and go once. Don't try to make friends. Try to learn where the court is and what time people actually turn up. First contact is the whole win.

Week 2 — Go back to the same thing

Return to the exact session from week one, plus add one new activity. Repetition is the point: the second time you show up, someone will half-recognize you, and by the third they'll say hi. Start learning names. When a session ends, hang around for five minutes instead of bolting — that's when plans get made.

Week 3 — Turn a session into a plan

By now you'll have one or two people whose game and vibe fit yours. Make the specific ask: "Same time next week?" or "A few of us are hitting Saturday — want in?" This is where a casual regular becomes an actual contact. Trade numbers with the two people you'd genuinely want to play with again.

Week 4 — Lock a standing session

Convert your best connection into a recurring slot — a weekly hit, a Tuesday run, a Sunday pickup game. A standing session is the finish line, because it keeps producing hours of shared time with zero further effort. From here, your circle grows on its own: regulars bring friends, and one game becomes a group.

Which sports work best for this

Anything recurring will work, but some formats build familiarity faster:

  • Pickup team sports (basketball, soccer, volleyball) — built-in teamwork and constant rotation of people.
  • Run and cycling clubs — long stretches of side-by-side time, which is prime conversation territory.
  • Climbing gyms — natural downtime between climbs, belaying builds quick trust.
  • Rec leagues — the same team every week for a whole season; friendship almost by default.
  • Racquet sports — a standing hit with one person is the easiest recurring commitment to keep.

Common mistakes after a move

Two errors sink most people. The first is event-hopping — trying a different thing every week so you never build the repetition that friendships need. Pick two or three activities and go back to them. The second is waiting to feel ready — for your fitness to improve, for the season to start, for a friend to come with you. There's never a perfect time; there's just the next session. Go to that one.

Key takeaways

  • Friendship comes from repeated, unforced time — sport manufactures it.
  • Pick 2–3 recurring activities and return to the same ones.
  • Week 1 show up, week 2 return, week 3 make the ask, week 4 lock a standing session.
  • A standing weekly game is the goal — it compounds on its own.
  • Don't event-hop and don't wait to feel ready; go to the next session.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best way to meet people in a new city?
Recurring, low-pressure activities beat one-off events. Sports are ideal because they give you a built-in reason to meet, a shared focus so conversation isn't forced, and natural repetition — you see the same people every week, which is how acquaintances become friends.
Which sports are best for making friends?
Team and group activities with regular sessions — pickup basketball or soccer, run clubs, climbing gyms, rec leagues, and group fitness. They combine repetition with built-in teamwork, which builds familiarity faster than solo sports.
How long does it take to make friends in a new city?
Casual friendships take roughly 40–60 hours of shared time, and closer ones far more. Standing weekly sessions are how you bank those hours quickly — a few months of a weekly game gets you most of the way there.
I'm not very good at my sport — can I still do this?
Yes. Look for beginner-friendly or drop-in sessions where everyone's mixed level, and be upfront about it. Most groups welcome newcomers, and shared improvement is its own bonding experience.
