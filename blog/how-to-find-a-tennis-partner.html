How to find a tennis partner near you: 7 ways that work

The court is free, your racquet's in the car — the only missing piece is someone to hit with. Here's how to fix that for good.

MatchYard·7 min read·Updated July 2026

The fastest way to find a tennis partner near you is to stop broadcasting into the void ("anyone want to hit?") and start filtering by the three things that actually decide whether a match happens: level, location, and when you're both free. Nail those and a one-off hit turns into a standing weekly game. Miss any one and you get a no-show or a mismatch you quietly never repeat.

Below are seven ways to find someone to play with — ordered roughly from fastest to most effort — plus the part most guides skip: what to say, and how to turn a first hit into a habit.

First, get clear on your level

Before you look, be able to describe how you play in one line. It saves everyone a wasted hour. If you know your NTRP or UTR rating, use it. If you don't, a plain description works just as well: "I can rally 10+ balls, serve goes in about half the time, still shaky at net." Honesty here is the single biggest predictor of a good match — over-selling your level is the most common reason a promising hit never becomes a second one.

1. Use a tennis partner app

Purpose-built apps are the highest-hit-rate option because they filter by level and availability up front. You set your rating, your home courts, and the times you actually play; the app surfaces people who overlap on all three. This is exactly the coordination problem MatchYard is built to solve — you skip the "are you any good / when are you free / where do you play" back-and-forth because it's already answered in the profile.

2. Post on the right community boards

Most clubs, rec centers, and public courts still run a physical or Facebook noticeboard. They work — but only if your post is specific. "Looking to hit" gets ignored. "3.5 player, weekday mornings, Eastside Park, want a regular Tuesday/Thursday hit" gets replies because the reader can instantly tell if it fits them.

3. Join a local ladder or box league

Parks-and-rec departments and clubs run ladders where you're ranked and challenge people near you. It's the best-value option going: you get a steady stream of level-matched opponents, a reason to actually schedule, and zero awkwardness about asking — the format does the asking for you.

The best tennis partner isn't the best player you can find. It's the one who's a little better than you, lives 10 minutes away, and is free when you are.

4. Take a group clinic or drop-in

A weekly clinic is a partner-finding machine disguised as a lesson. You're grouped by level automatically, you rotate through everyone, and by week three you'll know exactly who you want to book a private hit with. If you're new to an area, this is the lowest-pressure way in.

5. Ask your existing network — specifically

Someone you already know plays, or knows someone who does. The trick is the specific ask: not "does anyone play tennis?" but "I'm looking for a 3.0–3.5 partner for Sunday mornings near downtown — who comes to mind?" Specific questions jog specific memories.

6. Try Meetup and local sports groups

Meetup and neighborhood sports groups are strongest for social, mixed-level play and for meeting a batch of people at once. They're less precise on level-matching, so treat them as a way to widen your circle, then pair off with the people whose game fits yours.

7. Show up at the courts at peak times

The oldest method still works. Weekend mornings and weekday evenings, public courts fill with people in exactly your situation. Bring a can of balls, be friendly, and offer to fill in. One good hit with a regular can plug you into an entire existing group.

The mistake most people make

They find one partner and stop. Partners move, get injured, or drift. Keep two or three people in rotation from day one — that's the difference between "I play sometimes" and "I play every week."

What to say in your first message

Whatever channel you use, the message that gets a "yes" has three parts: your level, your availability, and a concrete suggestion. Compare these two:

  • Ignored: "Hey, do you want to play tennis sometime?"
  • Answered: "4.0-ish, free most weekday evenings — want to hit at Riverside Courts Thursday at 6?"

The second one gives the other person a decision they can make in five seconds. Always propose a specific court and time; let them counter if it doesn't work.

How to turn one hit into a standing game

The magic isn't finding a partner — it's not having to find one again next week. At the end of a good session, lock the next one before you leave the court: "Same time next Thursday?" A recurring slot beats motivation every time, because neither of you has to decide again. Within a month, that standing hit becomes the appointment you plan the rest of your week around.

Key takeaways

  • Match on level, location, and availability — not just proximity.
  • Be able to describe your level in one honest sentence before you reach out.
  • Apps and ladders have the highest hit-rate because they filter for you.
  • First messages win when they include a level, a time, and a specific court.
  • Keep 2–3 partners in rotation, and book the next game before you leave.

Frequently asked questions

How do I find a tennis partner at my level?
Filter by rating, not just location. Share your NTRP or UTR — or a plain description like "consistent rallies, still working on my serve" — so the person you meet plays at a level that keeps games competitive and fun. A partner slightly above you improves your game fastest.
Where can I find a tennis partner for free?
Public courts, community boards, tennis apps, Facebook groups, Meetup, and your local parks-and-rec ladder are all free or low cost. The fastest results come from tools that match you by level and availability rather than open "anyone want to hit?" posts.
What should I say in a first message to a tennis partner?
Keep it short and specific: your level, when you're free, and a concrete suggestion — for example, "4.0-ish, free weekday evenings, want to hit at Riverside Thursday at 6?" Specific offers get replies because they're easy to say yes to.
How often should I play to actually improve?
Once a week keeps your feel; twice a week is where noticeable improvement happens. A standing partner is what makes that consistency realistic — you're far more likely to show up when someone's expecting you.
