Pickup basketball etiquette: 12 unwritten rules of the court

Nobody hands you the rulebook at the park. Here's the one that actually gets you invited back to the run.

MatchYard·6 min read·Updated July 2026

Pickup basketball etiquette is the invisible system that keeps a court running smoothly without a referee, a scoreboard, or anyone officially in charge. Learn it and you become a regular — someone the court is glad to see. Ignore it and you'll feel a quiet freeze-out you can't quite name. These are the twelve unwritten rules that matter most, from calling next to calling fouls.

Getting on the court

1. Call "next" out loud, early

The single most important rule. When you arrive, announce "I've got next" to the court so everyone knows the order. Next goes in the order it was called — if someone claimed it before you, you're behind them. No list, no argument, just say it clearly and remember who's ahead of you.

2. Winners stay, losers sit — usually

On a busy court, the winning team keeps playing and the team with "next" comes on to challenge them. On a quieter court, house rules vary. When in doubt, ask "how's the court running today?" — one question saves a lot of confusion.

3. Whoever has next picks the team

If you called next, you fill your squad from the players waiting. Pick to win, but don't leave someone stranded game after game — spreading run keeps the court full, and a full court is a better court.

During the game

4. Call your own fouls — and only real ones

There's no ref, so the fouled player makes the call and it stands. The etiquette is restraint: call contact that actually affected the play, not every bump. The player who calls a foul on every possession is the player nobody wants on the court.

The best pickup players aren't the ones who score the most. They're the ones who make the game easy to keep playing.

5. Check the ball, and mean it

Possession starts with a check at the top of the key. Hand or bounce it to the defender, wait for "check" back, then go. Don't quick-snap it to catch someone off guard — that's the mark of someone who wins arguments and loses invitations.

6. Know the score before you shoot

Games are usually to 11 or 21, ones and twos, win by two. Call the score out loud at the top of each possession so there's no dispute at game point. If you're not sure, ask before the ball's live, not after the shot goes in.

7. Play defense — actually

Everyone wants to score; a run only works when people also guard. Pick someone up, stay in front of them, and don't cherry-pick. Reliable defenders get picked first for the next game every single time.

8. Pass to the open player

Hero ball empties a court faster than rain. Move the ball, reward the cut, and the four other people on your team will want to run with you again.

Being someone people want to run with

9. Match your intensity to the run

A Sunday-morning social run and a competitive evening run are different games. Read the room. Bringing playoff intensity to a casual shootaround — or coasting through a serious run — both read as not paying attention.

10. Own your mistakes, skip the theatrics

Air-balled it? Turned it over? "My bad" and move on. No slammed balls, no lingering arguments over a call. Emotional control is a skill, and on the court it's a visible one.

11. Respect the regulars and the newcomer

If a court has a crew that's been running for years, show a little deference — learn the house rules before you rewrite them. And when you're the regular, make room for the new person. Every regular was once the stranger standing on the sideline hoping to get picked.

12. Leave it better than you found it

Take your bottles, don't hog a hoop when people are waiting, and thank people for the run. Small stuff — but it's exactly the stuff that turns a random court into your court.

The mistake most people make

Trying to prove themselves by scoring. On a pickup court, reputation is built on reliability — playing defense, making the extra pass, and being easy to play with. Do that and you'll never have to ask to get in a game again.

Key takeaways

  • Call "next" out loud and early; go in the order it was called.
  • The fouled player calls the foul — call real ones only, and keep it moving.
  • Check the ball, call the score, and play honest defense.
  • Match your intensity to the run and control your emotions.
  • Reliability, not scoring, is what gets you picked first next time.

Frequently asked questions

How do you call next in pickup basketball?
Announce it out loud — "I've got next" — as soon as you arrive, and again as the current game nears its end. If someone claimed it before you, you're after them. Whoever has next fills their team from the players waiting.
Who calls fouls in pickup basketball?
The player who was fouled calls it, and the call generally stands. Call only real fouls that affected the play, keep it quick, and don't argue every bump — over-calling is the fastest way to not get invited back.
What are the basic rules of pickup basketball?
Games are usually to 11 or 21 by ones and twos, win by two. Call your own fouls, check the ball at the top to start, call the score each possession, and winners stay on. Confirm the local house rules before tip-off.
